\section{Introduction}
The automatic understanding of visual content has become one of the central challenges in modern computer vision and computer vison applications. In the current document we will describe a computer vision problem in the field of fashion industry, which we aim to solve by using deep learning methods.
In particular, the fashion industry increasingly relies on intelligent systems capable of analyzing clothing-and fashion items from images for applications such as online retail search, recommendation systems, inventory management, and trend analysis. Manual annotation of fashion products is time-consuming, costly, and prone to inconsistency, motivating the development of automated methods that can recognize fashion items and describe their visual attributes and characteristics.

\section{Dataset}

To train and evaluate our model, we use the DeepFashion2 dataset \cite{ge2019deepfashion2}.
DeepFashion2 is a large collection of clothing images containing 491K images and 801K
individual clothing items across 13 categories: short-sleeved shirt, long-sleeved shirt,
short-sleeved outwear, long-sleeved outwear, vest, sling, shorts, trousers, skirt,
short-sleeved dress, long-sleeved dress, vest dress, and sling dress. The dataset is
divided into a training set (391K images), a validation set (34K images), and a test
set (67K images).

The images come in two types: commercial images, which are clean photos taken in
controlled settings such as online shops, and consumer images, which are real-world
photos taken by people in everyday conditions. This distinction is important for our
project, as we aim to process photos that users take of their own wardrobe, which
closely resemble consumer images. Figure~\ref{fig:grid} shows a selection of images
from the validation set, illustrating the variety of clothing categories and image
conditions present in the dataset.

Each clothing item in the dataset is annotated with a bounding box, which is a
rectangle surrounding the item, a category label indicating the type of clothing,
and a pixel-level segmentation mask tracing the exact outline of the garment.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mask}, the segmentation mask makes it possible to
distinguish the garment from its background, which is the key annotation that
enables our digital wardrobe pipeline.

This dataset supports four tasks: clothes detection, landmark estimation, instance
segmentation, and consumer-to-shop retrieval. For our project, we focus
on instance segmentation, where the model takes an RGB image as input and produces,
for each detected clothing item, a predicted bounding box, a category label, and a
pixel-level segmentation mask.

The dataset presents several challenges relevant to our setting. Over 50\% of items
are partially or heavily occluded, more than 30\% are partially outside the image
frame, and items vary widely in scale. These variations reflect the conditions under
which users photograph their own clothing, and training on such a diverse dataset
makes the model more likely to generalise to real wardrobe photos.
